# Introduction

When the talent-loaded Celtics added all-stars Kyrie Irving and Gordon Hayward, they seemed to be on the verge of dominating the league for years to come. What transpired is the complete opposite. It was only a matter of weeks before the Boston dream shattered and their locker room turned into a frenzy. 

NBA teams are quick to jump at the opportunity to add star players, thinking more is unequivocally better. But that is not always the case. New signings can shake up a team and cause an abrupt end to an otherwise lengthy winning run. Teams who explore the potential of their current players, while making strategic signings to fill specific gaps, are better-suited for long-term success.

The league's greatest franchises were built that way. The 90s Bulls, the 80s Lakers, the 2000 Spurs all focused on building a system instead of chasing talent in free agency. Their main concern was building a systems for developing players to fit the kind of offense they wished to run. That made them target certain skillsets in free agency and the draft. This mindset is more conducive for long-term success, whereas teams who chase instant gratification are typically left scrambling shortly after. 

There is a point after which adding more stars can hurt, in which case at least one of them will have to take a backseat. History may provide the biggest lesson as to the underrated role of continuity and organic team building.
